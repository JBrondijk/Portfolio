Crafting Experiences

VISION ON DESIGN

As technological advancements continue to push the boundaries of what we can do, new ways to utilize these technologies are constantly being explored. Some people picture the futures of these technologies as a replacement for real, physical experiences.

I disagree, I believe physical experiences will continue to have their place in the ever more technological world. That isn’t to say that these physical experiences are enough however. Technology can enhance them, adding interactions unattainable in real life, and making these experiences more engaging and entertaining. Technologies like Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR) provide new ways to create immersive, autonomous interactive learning experiences that can be accessed from the classroom, home or anywhere else.

Take zoos as an example. The main draw of a zoo visit for most people is the experience of viewing wild animals. The public sees zoos as a form of entertainment, akin to theme parks. Zoos, on the other hand, would like to be seen more like a museum, with a focus on education. Using AR or VR, educational activities can be made more entertaining, bringing values of guests and zoos closer together.

Making these experiences is no simple task. The technology used is only a small part of the final experience, it’s up to designers to create value in the application of these technologies. Balancing the affordances offered by physical elements with those offered by technology.

The more technology gets involved, the more skills designers need to be able to design the experiences. Designing these games requires skills in programming, aesthetic design, user experience design, 3D modeling, animation, and depending on the kind of experience, storytelling, sound design, and much more. It’s impossible to be an expert at all of these skills, but knowing which skills are required to create the experience you’re trying to make, and familiarizing yourself with them, helps in creating cohesive designs, where each area compliments the other.

I strongly believe that providing this kind of “experiential education” will help people, young and old, be more motivated to learn. I see a future where they are common place in any learning environment, like classrooms, zoos, or museums.

Designer with an eye for detail

MY PROFESSIONAL IDENTITY

When I was still in primary school, my teacher would try to let us learn by doing activities, whenever possible. For biology class, we would head into the forest to learn about plants and ecosystems. It was fun, engaging and something I found to be lacking during high school, where I rarely felt as engaged to learn.

Now, as a designer, I’m a dedicated to using technology to create immersive learning experiences. In particular, experiences where users have a form of autonomy, allowing for self-expression and exploration within the environment of the learning experience.

What drives me in my while designing is not just making part of a product, but how this part contributes to the full experience. I want the full experience to be cohesive, and well thought out. I care for every detail, no matter how small, that might benefit the end user experience. I try to take every use case into account, planning out my designs in great detail, from start to finish. I draw a lot of inspiration from the videogames I enjoy. As a player, I’m often blown away by little details developers add to make their worlds feel alive. I love putting that level of care into the games I make as well, though I do have to be mindful not to be carried away with, prioritizing the most important aspects above all.

It’s important to involve users into the design process, to know who you’re designing for, and whether your designed solution is clear and effective. I try to learn who my users are, and use these insights to put myself in their shoes while designing. I’ll identify any problems and solve any problems that come up while designing or prototyping. This lets users give meaningful comments on the core of the design, rather than these surface-level problems, when discussing prototypes.

I am very capable of translating my designs into a fully experiential prototype, giving potential users something tangible to give feedback on. Over the past years, I’ve created several educational VR, AR and PC games. Each in-depth experiences featuring fully-fledged environments, intuitive controls, animated characters, visuals, sound, gameplay elements, and learning goals. I enjoy learning new skills I need when developing prototypes. I’m a quick and eager learner, picking up many skills over the past years, like programming in a variety of languages, drawing, 3D modeling, animating and video editing.
