There’s already countless ways to craft an experience. As technological advancements continue to push the boundaries of what we can do, even more is becoming possible. It’s hard to imagine life without smartphones nowadays, and this isn’t going to change. What we can do however, is go along with this flow, using smart phones to create experiences that are easily accessible, at a low cost.
Lots of stories about VR picture people living out their lives in some virtual world, a replacement for the reality. Personally, I think real, physical experiences will continue to have their place in the ever more technological world. That isn’t to say that these physical experiences are enough however. Technology can enhance physical experiences, adding interactions not possible in real life, making these experiences more engaging and entertaining.
Take zoos as an example. The main draw of a zoo visit for people is the experience of seeing wild animals. The public sees zoos as a form of entertainment, akin to theme parks. Zoos, on the other hand, would like to be seen more like a museum, with a focus on education. Using AR or VR, educational activities can be made more entertaining as well, bringing values of guests and zoos closer together.
These approaches can be used in other settings as well. Like in museums, or even cities, which sometimes try to educate about landmarks. Sometimes there isn’t even a need for education, like in theme parks, where the full focus can be of the entertainment these technologies offer.
It’s up to designers to find out how these experiences can be created. The more technology gets involved, the more skills designers need to be able to design them. Designing games requires skills in storytelling, aesthetic design, user experience, empathy, architecture, sound design, programming, visual arts and much more, depending on the game. Being skilled in as many of these areas as possible, helps in creating cohesive designs, where each area compliments the other.
